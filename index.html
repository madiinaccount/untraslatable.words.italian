Untranslatable Italian Words

Italian isn’t just about ciao and grazie, as every other language in the world, some of its words carry meanings and emotions that are impossible to translate directly into English.

Curious? You’re in the right place!

If you’ve been studying the language for a while, you’ve probably noticed that some Italian words don’t behave the way you expect them to. You look them up in a dictionary, you find a translation, but when you hear them used by native speakers, something still feels missing. That’s because not all words can be translated directly. Some of them carry cultural meanings, emotions, and social habits that don’t exist in the same way in English.

This website is for non-native speakers of Italian who are curious about those “untranslatable” words. Here you’ll find a small collection of expressions that Italians use in everyday life, especially in informal conversations. Instead of giving you a single English equivalent, each entry explains how the word is actually used, what kind of situations it belongs to, and what it says about Italian culture. The goal is not to help you memorize a definition, but to help you recognize these words when you hear them and feel more confident using them yourself.

By exploring these entries, you’ll start to see Italian less as a set of rules and more as a living language shaped by culture and daily life. And who knows, you might even surprise your Italian friends by using one of these words at just the right moment.

Aperitivo

A social meal that combines alcoholic or non-alcoholic drinks and dinner-like snacks
Part of speech: Noun
Register: Informal
Definition in Italian: Pasto informale che unisce una bevanda alcolica, o analcolica, con stuzzichini.
Cultural / pragmatic note: Imagine finishing your workday and wanting to relax with friends. You don’t feel like cooking a full dinner, but you also don’t want to just go out for a drink. This is where aperitivo comes in. It’s a typical Italian habit that combines a drink with simple food that sometimes replaces dinner.

On the table, you might find a selection of salumi such as prosciutto and salami, cheese, a few olives, a quiche, and some focaccia or slices of pizza. Nothing that requires hours in the kitchen, just enough food to share, snack on, and enjoy with a drink in hand. Wine, spritz or Crodino (a non-alcoholic beverage) flow freely as conversation and laughter fill the air.

The atmosphere is relaxed and social. Friends gather around a table or a counter, chat about their day, and nibble from shared plates. The focus is on connection and enjoyment, not on formality or a structured meal. For many Italians, aperitivo is the perfect way to end the day and combine food with social life.
Example sentence: Facciamo un aperitivo dopo il lavoro?

Gattara

A woman stereotypically associated with caring for many cats
Part of speech: Noun
Register: Colloquial
Definition in Italian: Donna che si prende cura di numerosi gatti.
Cultural / pragmatic note: A gattara is a woman who dedicates a lot of time to taking care of cats. Often, she feeds stray cats, cleans their spaces, and sometimes rescues kittens from the streets. Stereotypically, she may have several cats at home, sometimes ranging from just a few to a dozen. She clearly loves them and the term itself comes from this very visible and recognizable habit. However, the word carries more than simple affection for animals.

Gattara often has a slightly negative or ironic tone. The stereotype associated with it portrays a woman whose life revolves almost entirely around her cats, to the point that she is imagined as ending up alone, without a partner or family, living surrounded only by her animals. The word is highly gendered and reflects a broader cultural bias. Men who care for many cats are rarely labeled in the same way, because they are not perceived through the same social expectations. In this sense, gattara can be considered implicitly sexist, as it targets women and reinforces stereotypes about female loneliness and failed relationships, ending up being somehow incomplete or socially questionable.

Despite this critical undertone, gattara is mostly colloquial and often used with humor or light mockery rather than open hostility. Italians may laugh or shake their heads when using the word, recognizing both the woman’s dedication and the social judgment implied by the label.
Example sentence: Nel quartiere la conoscono tutti come la gattara.

Menefreghista

Someone who doesn’t care about rules, consequences, or others
Part of speech: Noun / Adjective
Register: Colloquial, pejorative
Definition in Italian: Persona che mostra totale indifferenza.
Cultural / pragmatic note: In Italian, a menefreghista is someone who simply doesn’t care; about rules, about consequences, or about how their actions affect other people. The term is often used to describe a person who is selfish, indifferent, or irresponsibly unconcerned with the feelings of those around them. It is a colloquial and slightly pejorative word, so calling someone a menefreghista can be humorous, ironic, or openly critical, depending on the context.

Italians commonly use it to point out a lack of empathy or civic sense, whether they are referring to a friend who doesn’t return a favor, a colleague who ignores responsibilities, or a stranger behaving inconsiderately in public. So, it is not a neutral label: it usually expresses disapproval, sometimes softened by irony or playful teasing. A menefreghista might do something as small as leaving trash on the street without a second thought, or something more serious, like ignoring someone’s emotional needs.

One important note is that the word does not change according to gender. Whether you are talking about a man or a woman, menefreghista remains the same form. The term is closely tied to social observation: it doesn’t describe indifference in general, but rather self-centered behavior at the expense of others. For non-native speakers, learning this word is useful not only to understand its meaning, but also its tone.
Example sentence: È un menefreghista, pensa solo a sé stesso.

Abbiocco

Post-meal sleepiness, especially after lunch
Part of speech: Noun
Register: Informal
Definition in Italian: Sonnolenza intensa dopo un pasto.
Cultural / pragmatic note: Abbiocco is the feeling of strong sleepiness that comes after eating, usually after a big meal. It’s a common experience, especially after lunch, when the combination of food, digestion, and relaxation can make your eyelids feel heavy. The word is informal and often used humorously. Italians might say they have l’abbiocco to explain why they are dozing off or moving slowly after a meal. It doesn’t refer to tiredness in general, but specifically to this post-meal drowsiness, often followed by a short nap, a riposino.

Abbiocco is deeply tied to Italian daily life and habits. Lunch is usually the main meal of the day, and many people enjoy it with several courses or hearty portions. Experiencing abbiocco is normal and culturally accepted; it’s even joked about among friends and family. In short, it captures a very Italian moment: the relaxed, almost sleepy period after enjoying a meal. Knowing this word helps learners recognize when Italians talk about post-meal naps and understand the playful way they describe ordinary life experiences.

Example sentence: Dopo pranzo mi viene sempre l’abbiocco.

Cornuto

Someone whose romantic partner has been unfaithful
Part of speech: Noun / Adjective
Register: Colloquial, pejorative
Definition in Italian: Persona tradita dal partner in una relazione sentimentale.
Cultural / pragmatic note: In Italian, cornuto is used to describe someone whose romantic partner has been unfaithful. It literally means “horned,” and the word comes from medieval European traditions and popular imagery, where horns symbolized betrayal in love and the public humiliation of the betrayed partner. Over time, this image became rooted in jokes, sayings, and everyday language.

The term is colloquial and often pejorative, so it can be insulting if used directly. At the same time, it appears frequently in Italian humor, media, and casual conversation, sometimes jokingly and sometimes with a more biting tone. Being called cornuto evokes not only the act of betrayal itself, but also the embarrassment, gossip, and social judgment that can follow infidelity.

The word is used for both men and women. While cornuto is the masculine form and cornuta the feminine, the meaning remains the same: a person who has been cheated on by their partner. Its use for women is less stereotyped than for men, but it is still common in everyday speech and carries the same mix of irony, mockery, and social commentary. In both cases, the focus is not only on cheating, but on how infidelity is perceived and talked about within a community. Knowing cornuto helps learners understand an important aspect of Italian language and culture: the way personal relationships, betrayal, and public perception are often discussed openly, sometimes humorously and sometimes cruelly, through vivid and emotionally charged words.

Example sentence: Tutti sapevano del tradimento, tranne il povero cornuto.
